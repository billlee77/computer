The Parabolic Section

This is the cone defined by the equation: x2 + y2 - z2 = 0 together with the plane x + z/3 = 3, whose intersection defines the curve in two variables. In order to best see this curve in the "natural x-y plane", we find the transformation that rotates the plane so that in these new coordinates, the equation of the plane is of the form z' = constant. The required transformation is:

Graphing Functions and Derivatives

Graphing a function along with it's first and second derivatives just from the shape of the original function. In this case, the function (in red) is y = 24/(x2 + 12). Since the slope of the function is zero at x=0, the first derivative function must have a value y=0 at x=0 If you imagine sliding a tangent line along the red curve at each point from the left to the right, it starts out nearly flat, then rises to a maximum, somewhere between -4 and zero, having a value zero at x=0. This produces the left half of the curve in green. Similarly for the right half. The second derivative curve follows from reapplying this trick over again, to give you a curve that has the general shape in gold.

The Math/Gym Analogy

It's easy to fall behind in schools, particularly in a subject area such as Math or Science. After a student falls behind, it becomes a frightening ordeal as new material is presented, and the student struggles to keep up with the material they're supposed to know. Pretty soon the student drops out and may develop resentment for the subject and teachers. Sometimes they think they're not smart enough, and develop math anxiety, and avoid anything that uses any math. Ultimately, the student also avoids careers that use any Math. This is certainly not the answer, especially in today high tech world.

The truth is that anyone can be good in math. However it does take some regular time devoted in developing the basic skills that will serve as a foundation for the higher levels, and ultimately greater career choices. Math like athletics, requires structure and discipline.

Math is one subject that is best approached a little at a time and with regular workout intervals. Even as little as 15 minutes a day in focused daily problem solving can dramatically increase one's proficiency, confidence and remove that 'pit in the stomach' that many have developed over their past lack of success.

It's also important that goals be set. Some goals are more long term than others. Improving your grade is may be your goal in the short run, while a longer goal might be acquire a better understanding of the subject. This depends on how much the student commits themselves to achieving the goal. As long as you have a goal, I will help you create a plan, where and how much to spend your time in given topic area.

Summary:

  • Learn the rules of the game
  • Reinforce these rules through exercises. We learn by repetition. Work out similar problems until it becomes second nature
  • Computation first, understanding comes more naturally later (don't and try to understand too much at first)

The Tutor/Coach

Having a good tutor or coach who can pay attention to the individual problems and help the student identify the problem area is crucial. A tutor serves not only to present the material to the student, but should be perceptive to the students own needs, shortcomings, and fears. Based on the level the student is at, a tutor should provide an overview and plan in a general area, that the student would use over some time (perhaps after the tutor is no longer with the student), to continually gain greater proficiency.

The tutor/student relationship should be one of personal comfort. The student should feel unencumbered and unafraid to confide in any fears they have on the subject. This should be quite unlike many of the peer-peer relationship in school, where students are often afraid to ask questions or even volunteer an answer for fear of ridicule, even though they themselves probably have the same questions.

I do however stress problem solving at regular intervals. Initially, many of the skills may be rote problem solving that test the techniques, rather than how smart the student really is. Depending on the student, understanding gradually comes. Over time, as the student learns to understand why these techniques produces correct the answers, they are more likely to remember and reconstruct arguments long after their detailed memory has failed.

When I meet you for the first time, we will spend the first fifteen minutes (at no extra charge) getting to know each other and what are each of our expectations.
